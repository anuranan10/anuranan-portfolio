Mission Context

Geostationary Earth Orbit satellites form the backbone of global communications, weather monitoring, and data-relay networks. NASA’s Tracking and Data Relay Satellite System (TDRS) enables continuous contact between LEO spacecraft and ground stations — but reaching GEO from a 300 km parking orbit requires a precise, multi-burn ascent that consumes the majority of a spacecraft’s propellant. This project simulated the full orbital trajectory and maneuver sequence for a TDRS-M-class spacecraft: from LEO insertion through a Hohmann transfer to GTO, to final GEO circularization and inclination reduction, with an additional phasing maneuver designed to correct a 5° longitudinal slot error.

  • Propagate a 300 km circular parking orbit via two-body equations of motion integrated with ode45
  • Compute Δv requirements for the full LEO → GTO → GEO sequence, including a 28.5° plane change
  • Estimate propellant mass and mass fraction using the Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation (Isp = 320 s)
  • Extract Classical Orbital Elements (COEs) at every major mission stage
  • Design and validate a phasing maneuver to synchronize two co-GEO spacecraft separated by 5°
  • Benchmark all results against real TDRS-M (2017) mission data

Phase 1 — LEO Parking Orbit Propagation

The spacecraft begins at r0 = RE + 300 = 6678 km in a circular equatorial orbit. Circular velocity follows directly from the gravitational parameter μE = 398,600.418 km3/s2:

Vcirc = √(μ/r0) = 7.73 km/s

The initial Cartesian state r(0) = [6678, 0, 0] km and v(0) = [0, 7.73, 0] km/s was integrated for one full orbital period T = 2π√(r03/μ) = 5400 s using ode45 at RelTol = AbsTol = 10−9. Position magnitude error over the full orbit was less than 10−5 km, confirming numerical stability consistent with mission-grade analysis.

The parking orbit Classical Orbital Elements (COEs): a = 6678 km, e = 0, i = 28.5°, Ω = 0°, ω = 0°, θ = 0°. The trajectory lies entirely in the equatorial plane as expected for a zero-inclination initial state.

Phase 2 — Hohmann Transfer (LEO → GTO)

A Hohmann transfer ellipse connects the parking orbit perigee (rp = 6678 km) to GEO altitude (ra = 42164 km). Transfer orbit velocities at perigee and apogee were computed from the vis-viva equation:

v = √[μ(2/r − 1/a)]

Key computed values:

  • Transfer semi-major axis: at = (6678 + 42164) / 2 = 24421 km
  • Transfer perigee velocity: Vp,t = 10.15 km/s
  • Transfer apogee velocity: Va,t = 1.61 km/s
  • Transfer orbit period: T = 10.95 hr   →   time to apogee: tPA = 5.48 hr
  • First burn (injection into GTO): Δv1 = 10.15 − 7.73 = 2.42 km/s

Phase 3 — GEO Insertion & Plane Change

At GEO apogee the spacecraft must simultaneously circularize its orbit and reduce inclination from 28.5° to 0°. Performing the plane change here — where velocity is lowest at 3.07 km/s — is critical. The same plane change executed at LEO perigee (10.15 km/s) would cost approximately 5.00 km/s, rendering the mission propellant budget infeasible.

Circularization and inclination burns analyzed separately:

Δvcirc = VGEO − Va,t = 3.07 − 1.61 = 1.46 km/s

Δvinc = 2 · VGEO · sin(Δi / 2) = 2(3.07)sin(14.25°) = 1.50 km/s

Total mission Δv = 2.42 + 1.46 + 1.50 = 5.36 km/s.

Propellant mass estimation via the Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation (m0 = 3500 kg, Ve = Isp · g0 = 3.139 km/s):

m0 / mf = eΔv/Ve = e5.36/3.139 = 5.53   →   mf = 633 kg, mp = 2867 kg (82%)

Phase 4 — GEO Phasing Maneuver

An interceptor spacecraft leading its target by Δθ0 = 5° in GEO needs to slow its angular drift and wait for the target to catch up. This is achieved by briefly raising the orbit into a phasing ellipse with a longer period. The required semi-major axis satisfies:

Tphase / TGEO = 1 + Δθ / 2π

Computed phasing orbit parameters:

  • Phasing semi-major axis: aphase = 42554 km
  • Phasing apogee radius: ra = 42943 km (758 km above GEO)
  • Maneuver duration: 24.3 hours
  • Burn 1 (enter phasing orbit): Δv1 = 0.014 km/s
  • Burn 2 (return to GEO): Δv2 = 0.014 km/s
  • Total phasing cost: 0.028 km/s — less than 0.6% of the total mission Δv budget

Numerical propagation confirmed the relative longitude decreased smoothly from 5° to 0° over the 24.3-hour window, with ode45 tracking both spacecraft simultaneously in the ECI frame.

MATLAB Code

Two simulation scripts formed the core analysis. The parking orbit propagator used a local twobody function passed to ode45. The phasing script computed orbit geometry analytically and then integrated both spacecraft simultaneously to verify longitude convergence.

% AE 313 — Parking Orbit Propagation
mu = 398600.4418;
r0 = [6678; 0; 0];
v0 = [0; sqrt(mu/6678); 0];
y0 = [r0; v0];
T  = 2*pi*sqrt(6678^3/mu);           % 5400 s

opts = odeset('RelTol',1e-9,'AbsTol',1e-9);
[t,y] = ode45(@(t,y) twobody(t,y,mu), [0 T], y0, opts);

plot(y(:,1), y(:,2), 'LineWidth', 1.5)
axis equal;  xlabel('x (km)');  ylabel('y (km)')

function dydt = twobody(~, y, mu)
    r = y(1:3);  v = y(4:6);
    a = -mu * r / norm(r)^3;
    dydt = [v; a];
end

% AE 313 — Phasing Maneuver Numerical Simulation
rG = 42164;
vG = sqrt(mu/rG);   nG = sqrt(mu/rG^3);
dth0 = deg2rad(5);

np     = nG / (1 + dth0/(2*pi));
Tphase = 2*pi / np;
ap     = (mu / np^2)^(1/3);          % 42554 km
ra     = 2*ap - rG;                  % 42943 km

vp_phase = sqrt(mu*(2/rG - 1/ap));
dv1 = vp_phase - vG;                 % 0.0140 km/s

fprintf('a_phase = %.1f km | ra = %.1f km\n', ap, ra)
fprintf('dv1 = %.4f km/s | Tphase = %.2f hr\n', dv1, Tphase/3600)
% Output: a_phase = 42553.5 km | ra = 42943.0 km
%         dv1 = 0.0140 km/s  | Tphase = 24.27 hr

Key Takeaways

Always perform plane changes at apogee.

The 28.5° inclination reduction cost 1.50 km/s at GEO altitude vs. ~5.00 km/s at LEO perigee — a 3.5 km/s savings from one sequencing decision. This rule is fundamental to every GEO mission burn design.

Launch vehicles do the heavy lifting in real missions.

TDRS-M’s Delta IV absorbed most of the inclination reduction through a supersynchronous GTO. The result: 42% propellant fraction vs. this simulation’s 82%. Optimizing the LV injection profile is as important as spacecraft propulsion design.

Phasing orbits are extremely fuel-efficient.

Correcting a 5° longitude error cost only 0.028 km/s — less than 0.6% of total mission Δv. This efficiency makes phasing the standard technique for GEO slot acquisition, satellite repositioning, and on-orbit rendezvous operations.

ode45 delivers mission-grade numerical accuracy.

Position error stayed below 10−5 km over a full 5400-second orbit with RelTol = AbsTol = 10−9. This solver-tolerance discipline is the same approach used in NASA preliminary orbit analysis tools and is essential for any simulation that feeds a propellant budget.
