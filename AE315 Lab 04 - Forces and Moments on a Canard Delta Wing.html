Lab 04: Forces and Moments on a Canard Delta Wing

AE 315 · Experimental Aerodynamics · Spring 2025 · ERAU

Experiment Overview

Delta wing and canard configurations appear on high-performance aircraft like the Eurofighter and Saab Gripen because they sustain lift at extreme angles of attack through leading-edge vortex lift — a mechanism conventional airfoils cannot exploit. This lab used the ERAU Micaplex closed-circuit wind tunnel’s six-component force balance to measure the complete aerodynamic load set on a canard-delta model across a wide operating envelope, giving direct exposure to how professional wind tunnel testing is conducted.

  • Measure all six aerodynamic loads: lift, drag, side force, and pitching, rolling, and yawing moments
  • Apply aerodynamic tares using the image–invert method to remove structural interference from force balance readings
  • Compare performance at two Reynolds numbers and two yaw angles across angles of attack from −4° to 34°
  • Build a drag polar and identify the best CL/CD operating point for each test condition

Equipment & Tools

  • ERAU Micaplex closed-circuit wind tunnel (75 fps and 100 fps test speeds)
  • Canard-delta wing model (Sw = 242.01 in², bw = 24.5 in, cw = 9.88 in)
  • WAFBC six-component external force balance (sting-mounted)
  • Aerodynamic tare hardware: image and invert mounting configurations
  • MATLAB for tare correction, coefficient computation, and polar plotting

Approach & Key Equations

The model was tested at four conditions — 75 fps and 100 fps, at 0° and 10° yaw — while angle of attack swept from −4° to 34° in 2° steps. Raw balance outputs were corrected using the image-invert tare procedure, then non-dimensionalized by dynamic pressure q and reference area Sw:

CL = Lift / (q·Sw)    CD = Drag / (q·Sw)    Cm = Pitch / (q·Sw·cw)

A quadratic curve fit to the drag polar (CD = CD0 + K·CL²) extracted the parasitic drag and induced drag factor K for each condition. Reynolds numbers were computed from the “Reynolds Number per ft” channel in the raw tunnel data.

Key Results

  • CL increased nearly linearly up to ~25°, then gradually rolled off — the signature of vortex lift breakdown on a delta wing rather than the abrupt stall of a conventional airfoil.
  • Higher Reynolds number (100 fps) produced marginally higher CL and lower CD, confirming that better flow attachment at higher Re improves overall efficiency.
  • 10° yaw had negligible effect on lift but measurably increased side force and yawing moment — important considerations for directional stability analysis.
  • Best CL/CD = 4.21 at 12° AoA (75 fps, 0° yaw). Higher yaw and higher Re both reduced the peak efficiency ratio.

Valuable Takeaways

  • Delta wings don’t stall abruptly — they tolerate high angles of attack gracefully. The gradual vortex lift roll-off above 25° is why delta configurations appear on fighters and supersonic aircraft that must operate across extreme flight regimes.
  • Tare corrections are essential for accurate force balance data. Without the image-invert method, structural interference from the mount hardware would contaminate every measurement — a fundamental discipline in professional wind tunnel testing.
  • The drag polar compresses all performance information into a single curve. Reading it to extract CD0, K, and the best-glide point is a core skill for aircraft sizing, mission analysis, and performance optimization.
  • Gained direct experience operating a closed-circuit facility, applying multi-variable test matrix design, and analyzing six-degree-of-freedom aerodynamic datasets — skills directly applicable to industry wind tunnel programs.
