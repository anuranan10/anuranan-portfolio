Lab 02: Flow Around Cylinders

AE 315 · Experimental Aerodynamics · Spring 2025 · ERAU

Experiment Overview

The cylinder is one of the most studied shapes in fluid mechanics because it captures bluff-body aerodynamics in a clean, symmetric geometry: stagnation, attached flow, boundary layer separation, and a turbulent wake. Understanding how pressure distributes around a blunt body — and how drag arises from that distribution — is directly relevant to designing fuselages, struts, landing gear, and external fuel tanks.

  • Measure the full surface pressure distribution around a cylinder at two speeds: 10 m/s (Re ≈ 23,500) and 15 m/s (Re ≈ 35,300)
  • Compute the pressure coefficient Cp at each angular position and compare against ideal potential-flow theory
  • Numerically integrate the pressure distribution to extract the drag coefficient Cd
  • Observe how increasing Reynolds number delays flow separation from the cylinder surface

Equipment & Tools

  • ERAU open-circuit wind tunnel
  • 1.4-inch aluminum cylinder with single static pressure port (24 angular positions, 0°–180°)
  • Differential pressure transducer and analog manometer
  • Angular rotation fixture for cylinder positioning
  • MATLAB for data acquisition, coefficient computation, and plotting

Approach & Key Equations

A 1.4-inch cylinder with a single static pressure port was rotated to 24 angular positions from 0° to 180°, capturing differential pressure at each angle for both test speeds. The pressure coefficient was found by normalizing each reading against the freestream dynamic pressure. Potential flow theory predicts Cp = 1 − 4sin²(θ), which was plotted alongside measured data for comparison.

Drag was extracted by integrating Cp·cos(θ) around the cylinder using MATLAB’s trapz function. This pressure-integration method converts the mapped surface pressures directly into a net streamwise force coefficient, requiring no physical force sensor:

Cd = ∫0π Cp cos(θ) dθ

Key Results

  • Below ~40°, experimental Cp closely matched potential flow near the stagnation point. Past ~40°, the measured pressure deviated sharply — the viscous boundary layer separated and the inviscid model no longer applied.
  • Both tests were subcritical (Re < 300,000): no drag crisis occurred and flow separated on the upstream portion of the cylinder at both speeds.
  • At 15 m/s, flow remained attached slightly longer than at 10 m/s — a direct demonstration that higher Reynolds number delays separation, reducing the low-pressure wake region.
  • Cd = 1.31 at 10 m/s and Cd = 1.42 at 15 m/s — drag coefficient increased with speed because a wider turbulent wake raised the pressure drag contribution.

MATLAB Code

Pressure data was loaded from 24 .mat files per test speed, converted to Pascals, and integrated to produce Cp and Cd. The potential flow reference was computed analytically for comparison.

% Pressure coefficient at 10 m/s
Cp_10 = dp_10ms / (0.5 * density * 10^2) + 1;

% Potential flow reference
Cp_potential = 1 - 4 .* sind(angles).^2;

% Drag coefficient via trapezoidal integration
theta = deg2rad(angles);
Cd_10 = trapz(theta, Cp_10 .* cos(theta));  % = 1.3055

Valuable Takeaways

  • Inviscid theory breaks down exactly where it matters most — at separation. The agreement near stagnation and divergence past 40° shows precisely where viscosity enters the picture, a boundary that governs drag on every real bluff body.
  • Drag can be extracted entirely from surface pressure data via integration, without a force balance. This method is used in wind tunnel testing and CFD validation to decompose drag into pressure and viscous components.
  • Reynolds number controls separation. Even the modest difference between 10 and 15 m/s produced measurable differences in flow attachment — reinforcing why scale effects must be considered when interpreting wind tunnel data for full-size aircraft.
  • Developed practical skills in angular pressure acquisition, numerical integration of surface distributions, and structured comparison of experimental versus analytical aerodynamic predictions.
